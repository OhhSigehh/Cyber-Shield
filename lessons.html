Intro Lessons

In today’s digital era, cybersecurity is a critical concern. Students rely heavily on online platforms for education, gaming, and communication. However, threats like phishing, malware, identity theft, and unauthorized access put them at risk. Even one careless click can compromise sensitive information.

  • Phishing: Fake messages tricking you into revealing personal data.
  • Malware: Malicious software that can damage your device or steal data.
  • Weak Passwords: Easy-to-guess logins that hackers can exploit.
  • Identity Theft: When attackers use your personal information for fraud.

Many students know about threats but fail to take strong protective steps. Awareness is only the first step—taking consistent action is what protects you in the long run.

  • Use strong, unique passwords.
  • Enable two-factor authentication.
  • Think before clicking links or downloading files.
  • Update your software regularly.
  • Be cautious with personal information online.

Everything you post, like, and share online creates a digital record. Recruiters, schools, and even hackers can analyze your history.
Tip:
Before posting, ask: “Will this affect me in 5 years?”

Hackers don’t just attack systems—they exploit people. Pretending to be IT staff, friends, or authority figures, they pressure victims into revealing secrets.

Rule:
Never share passwords or codes with anyone, even if they sound legit.

Modern AI can create convincing fake videos and voices. Students must learn to verify sources before believing or sharing content.
Tip:
Always cross-check suspicious information with trusted sites.

More Lessons

You already know that strong passwords matter — but even strong ones can be stolen. 2FA adds an extra lock, like a text code or authentication app. Even if hackers guess your password, they can’t get in without the second factor.

Action Step:
Enable 2FA on email, social media, and banking apps today. Think of it as having two keys for one door.
A school computer suddenly locks and shows: “Your files are encrypted. Pay $200 in Bitcoin to restore them.”
This is ransomware. Hackers encrypt files and demand money.
Protection:
Back up files regularly. Don’t pay — it doesn’t guarantee recovery.

What you share online can be used against you. Hackers collect birthdays, schools, and interests to guess passwords or impersonate you.

Action Step:
Review your Facebook, Instagram, or TikTok privacy settings. Limit who can see your posts, and avoid oversharing personal details.

Just like brushing teeth daily prevents cavities, cyber hygiene keeps your devices clean from attacks. It’s about consistent small habits: regular updates, password managers, safe browsing.

Action Step:
Schedule “Digital Clean-up Day” once a month — update apps, clear old accounts, and check privacy settings.
You realize your account was hacked yesterday. What’s your first move?
Steps:
  1. Change your password immediately.
  2. Enable 2FA.
  3. Notify friends if scam messages were sent from your account.
  4. Report to platform support.
The faster you act, the less damage hackers can do.
